# Brand Voice — Meridian Strength Coaching

## Who is the voice?

The voice is **Sarah Chen**, the founder. She's a former physical therapist turned strength coach — 15 years in women's health, direct, science-backed, warm but not saccharine. She writes like the most knowledgeable friend in your group chat: she'll tell you the truth, but she's on your side.

## Three pillars of the voice

1. **Direct** — Sarah doesn't hedge. She'll tell you the five-pound dumbbells aren't doing what you think they're doing.
2. **Warm** — she's not a drill sergeant. She assumes you're doing your best with what you know.
3. **Evidence-based** — every claim she makes has a study, a clinical experience, or a client outcome behind it.

## Voice IS

- Specific — Sarah uses exact numbers (weights, reps, weeks) not "heavy" or "soon"
- Direct but never shaming — she'll correct a myth without making the reader feel stupid for believing it
- Warm and peer-level — she writes like she's sitting across from you at a coffee shop
- Scientifically grounded but not academic — she references research without reading like a paper
- Anti-diet-culture — she rejects "toning," "bikini body," "bounce back," and all weight-loss-adjacent framing
- Funny in a dry way — never joke-y, but doesn't take itself too seriously
- Permission-giving — it's okay to want to get strong, okay to lift heavy, okay to not care about weight
- Patient — strength takes years, and she'll say that honestly

## Voice is NOT

- Shouty or bro-y ("LET'S GO LADIES", "CRUSH IT")
- Restriction-framed ("cut X," "avoid Y," "earn your meal")
- Body-shape-focused ("tone your arms," "sculpt your core")
- Motivational-poster-y ("YOU GOT THIS," "queen," "girl boss")
- Academic or jargon-heavy (no "hypertrophic stimulus" without translation)
- Wellness-vague ("holistic," "aligned," "journey")
- Age-pandering ("for women of a certain age")

## Sentence structure

- Average sentence length: mixed. Short for emphasis, medium for teaching, occasional long sentence when explaining a concept.
- Fragments: yes, sparingly. For landing a point.
- Contractions: always (you'll, don't, it's)
- Direct address: yes — "you" and "your" throughout
- Formatting moves: short paragraphs (1-3 sentences). Occasional bold for key terms. No excessive formatting.

## Signature phrases

- "Here's what the research actually shows..."
- "Five-pound dumbbells are not doing what you think they are."
- "You're not broken. You're undertrained."
- "Stronger, not smaller."
- "Progress is boring. Boring works."

## Opening moves

1. Drop into a specific moment with a client ("Last week, Rachel DM'd me asking if...")
2. Name a common myth in the first line and disprove it ("'I don't want to get bulky' — here's why that's not a risk")
3. Lead with a specific number or data point ("Women over 40 lose 3-5% of their muscle mass per decade. Here's what reverses it.")
4. Open with a reader question (real or representative)

## Closing moves

1. Direct CTA with no hedging ("Join the next cohort. Starts Monday.")
2. A reframe that reinforces the core message
3. A PS that answers the most common objection

## Tone variations

- **Promotional emails:** slightly more direct, slightly more urgent. Still warm, but less coffee-shop-friend and more "I know this works and I want you in it."
- **Nurture emails:** the full coffee-shop-friend voice. Teaching moments. Client stories. Myth-busting.
- **Announcements:** matter-of-fact. News first, context second.

## Gut-check questions

- Does this sound like Sarah, or does it sound like a fitness ad?
- Am I assuming the reader is smart?
- Did I give a specific number or a real example instead of a generalization?
- Am I correcting a myth without shaming the reader for believing it?
- Would a client I already have nod at this and forward it to a friend?
