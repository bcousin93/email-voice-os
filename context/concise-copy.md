# Concise Copy

The brevity-and-specificity guardrail. Pairs with the other two checks:

- `anti-ai-phrases.md` catches **AI tells** (banned words/patterns).
- `brand-voice.md` catches **off-voice** (sounds like someone else).
- This file catches **flab and vagueness** — the Zinsser failures.

Anchor: William Zinsser, *On Writing Well*. "Clutter is the disease of American writing. We
are a society strangling in unnecessary words." The cure is two moves, run on every draft:
**cut the words that do no work, and replace the abstract with the concrete.**

---

## The two passes

### Pass 1 — Cut clutter

Read each sentence and ask: if I delete this word, does the meaning change? If not, it goes.

Usual offenders to delete on sight:
- Qualifiers that hedge: *very, really, quite, rather, somewhat, basically, actually, just, in order to, kind of, a bit, pretty much*
- Throat-clearing run-ups: *the fact that, it's important to note that, what this means is, at the end of the day, when it comes to, in terms of*
- Redundant pairs: *plan ahead, end result, free gift, completely eliminate, past history, each and every*
- Adverbs propping up weak verbs: "ran quickly" → "sprinted"; "said angrily" → "snapped"

> "the fact that the welcome sequence was not really converting very well" →
> "the welcome sequence wasn't converting"

### Pass 2 — Make it concrete

Zinsser: the concrete noun beats the abstract one every time. Vague is the enemy of trust.

- "significant results" → "engagement jumped 38%"
- "a lot of founders" → "100 founders in Cabo"
- "various niches" → "a blood bank for cats and dogs"
- "improved performance" → name the metric and the number
- "recently" / "soon" → the actual date or window

If a claim has no number, name, or scene behind it, either make it specific or cut it. A
sentence that could belong to any company in the space is clutter wearing a tie.

---

## Rules of thumb

1. **Shorter is the default.** Say it in 8 words before you allow 15.
2. **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two "and"s, it's probably two sentences.
3. **Strong verbs, few adverbs.** The verb should carry the weight, not its modifier.
4. **Cut the warm-up.** Delete the first sentence and see if the second is a better opener. It usually is.
5. **Specific or gone.** Every claim earns its place with a number, a name, or a scene.
6. **Read it aloud.** Anywhere you'd never say it that way out loud, rewrite it.

---

## The test

Before shipping, the draft passes if:

- [ ] No sentence survives that loses nothing when deleted.
- [ ] No qualifier from the offender list above remains unless it's doing real work.
- [ ] Every general claim has a specific behind it (or has been cut).
- [ ] You could read it aloud to one person and it would sound like talking, not writing.

This is Braden's own bar in his published posts: "The bar is so low. Clear it." applies to the
copy itself. Eight words, two sentences, one number-free but vivid claim, and a command. Nothing
to cut. That's the target.
