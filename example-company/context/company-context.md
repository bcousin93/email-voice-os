# Company Context — Meridian Strength Coaching

## What you actually do

**One sentence:**
Meridian Strength Coaching teaches women 35-55 how to lift weights properly so they can be strong in their 60s, 70s, and beyond.

**Short paragraph:**
We're an online strength coaching business. Our flagship is the Meridian Method — a 12-week progressive lifting program with weekly coaching, form reviews, and a community of women doing the same work. We're not a fitness app, we're not a diet program, we're not a motivation service. We teach women how to get measurably stronger, with the clinical context that makes it safe and effective for mid-life bodies.

## The core belief

Women have been sold the wrong story about strength. The real risk is not "getting bulky" — the real risk is losing the muscle and bone density that lets you stay independent for the second half of your life. Strength training is preventative medicine. Most women find out too late.

## What you're against

We're against the wellness-industrial complex that sells women "toning," "sculpting," and "bouncing back" while delivering none of the strength they actually need. We're against diet culture. We're against the idea that a 45-year-old woman needs a different kind of training than a 25-year-old man — the programming shifts, but the fundamentals are the same: progressive overload, protein, recovery.

## Core philosophy / core frameworks

### Progressive Overload
The one principle that makes everything else work. You lift a little more this week than you did last week. Over months and years, a little more becomes a lot more. Most women have never been coached through this — which is why they plateau in week two of every program they try.

### The Strength Deposit
We think of every lifting session as a deposit into a retirement account, except the retirement is your ability to walk up stairs at 75. The interest compounds. You can't start depositing in your 70s; the math doesn't work. The deposit window is 35-55, and it's open now.

### Boring Works
The program works because it's repetitive. Squat, deadlift, press, row, pull. Week after week. Progress is boring. Boring works. Anything that promises novelty every session is selling entertainment, not training.

## Values in communication

1. **Evidence over intuition** — every claim has a study, a client outcome, or clinical experience behind it. No "I read somewhere..."
2. **Specificity over generality** — "deadlift 185 lbs" not "get strong." "87% of graduates" not "most people."
3. **Peer-level, not guru-level** — Sarah writes like a knowledgeable friend, not a wellness influencer
4. **Patience over urgency** — we sell multi-year strength, not 30-day results
5. **Anti-shame** — reader is never wrong for what they believed before; they just haven't had the right information

## The brand story (canonical version)

Sarah Chen spent 15 years as a physical therapist, and for most of them she was frustrated. Her patients — overwhelmingly women in their 40s, 50s, and 60s — would come in with preventable injuries: wrist fractures from catching themselves falling, back pain from lifting a grandkid wrong, hip replacements from bone density loss that started in perimenopause.

The research was clear: progressive strength training would have prevented most of it. But her patients had been told for 30 years that lifting weights was "for men," that cardio was enough, that five-pound dumbbells were the goal. By the time they showed up in her PT clinic, they were rebuilding from scratch.

So Sarah started teaching women how to lift — first in small in-person groups in Portland, then online. The Meridian Method has now coached over 400 women, most of whom had never touched a barbell before.

Meridian exists because the default story about women's fitness is wrong, and the cost of believing it shows up in your 70s. We teach strength because strength is the one thing that compounds. Start now. You'll thank yourself in 30 years.
