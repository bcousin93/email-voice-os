# Audience — Meridian Strength Coaching

## Primary audience

**Who they are in one sentence:**
Women 35-55 who have tried everything — Pilates, yoga, cardio classes, macro tracking — and are tired of being told to do more of the same. They're smart, professional, and starting to feel the effects of perimenopause or bone-density loss, and they're ready to actually get strong.

**Demographics:**
- Age range: 35-55 (sweet spot: 40-50)
- Income range: $100K-$300K household
- Life stage: Established careers, often parenting tweens/teens, increasingly aware of aging
- Geography: US/Canada, primarily urban and suburban

**Psychographics:**
- What they believe: their body is starting to change and they want to stop pretending that "just be more consistent with SoulCycle" is the answer
- What they fear: frailty in their 60s and 70s, being the grandma who can't pick up the grandkid, losing independence
- What they want: to feel physically capable, to like their body without it being about its shape, to age with strength
- What they reject: diet culture, toning talk, "bikini body" framing, bro-ish gym culture, anything that pathologizes their age

## What keeps them up at night

1. "My mom broke her hip at 72 and I don't want that to be me."
2. "I've been 'working out' my whole life and I don't feel strong."
3. "I don't know if what I'm doing is actually working."
4. "I'm tired of starting over every January."
5. "I feel invisible at the gym and I hate it."

## Language they use

- "I want to be able to pick up my grandkids"
- "I don't want to get bulky" (a myth they've been told, not a real preference)
- "I used to run marathons but my knees are shot"
- "I've been doing Pilates for years and I still feel weak"
- "I don't even know what heavy is supposed to feel like"
- "I want to feel strong, not small"

## Language they reject

- "Queen" / "girl boss" / "you got this"
- "Tone your arms" / "sculpt your core"
- "Drop 10 pounds by summer"
- "For women of a certain age"
- Bro-gym language ("gains," "beast mode")
- Pseudoscience ("detox," "cleanse," "reset")

## Permission to want what they want

- To lift heavy without apologizing
- To stop caring about the scale
- To take up space in a gym
- To train for strength instead of "for their age"
- To have a body that can do things, not a body that looks a certain way
- To not feel like strength training is only for 25-year-old men

## What they've already tried

- Pilates and barre (felt good, didn't build real strength)
- SoulCycle / Peloton (cardio-heavy, didn't address muscle loss)
- Home workout apps (Beachbody, Obé, etc. — felt okay but plateaued)
- Personal trainers at commercial gyms (often not trained for women in perimenopause)
- Macro tracking (exhausting, felt like a second job)
- CrossFit (too intense for some, bad cueing for mid-life joints)

## Proof points they respond to

- **Specific numbers** — "deadlifted 185 lbs at 47" beats "got stronger"
- **Client stories with names and ages** — "Rachel, 49, lifted her grandkid one-armed for the first time"
- **Medical/clinical framing** — bone density, hormone context, clinical experience
- **Longitudinal results** — "after 18 months in the program" lands harder than "in 8 weeks"
- **Honest timelines** — "strength takes years, here's the year-one milestone" builds more trust than promises of rapid change

## Segments

### Segment A: The Rebuilder
- Who: former athlete or active person (35-45) who fell off during kids/career and wants to come back
- Primary concern: "I used to be strong. I want that back."
- Best-performing angles: capability-focused, identity-focused, nostalgia for athletic self

### Segment B: The First-Timer
- Who: never seriously lifted (40-55), scared of the gym, starting to notice aging
- Primary concern: "I don't know where to start and I don't want to look like an idiot."
- Best-performing angles: beginner-friendly, myth-busting, permission-giving
