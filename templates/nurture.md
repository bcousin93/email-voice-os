# Nurture Template

A story-first email that builds trust, teaches something useful, and only mentions the offer as a light touch (or not at all). Use when you're depositing trust, not withdrawing it.

---

## When to use this template

- Between promo campaigns
- Onboarding sequences (first few emails after signup)
- Weekly/regular newsletter sends
- Teaching content that reinforces your frameworks
- Story-driven content that humanizes the brand

**Don't use this template for:**
- Active promo campaigns (use `promo.md`)
- Launches, news, or announcements (use `announcement.md`)
- Emails whose primary goal is conversion

---

## The core rule of nurture

**Give more than you take.** A nurture email should leave the reader smarter, entertained, or reframed — whether or not they buy anything today. The offer mention, if any, is a single low-pressure line, not a pitch.

---

## Required inputs

- **Core insight or idea:** the one thing the reader should walk away with
- **Story, example, or data:** the concrete material that illustrates the insight
- **Optional offer tie-in:** which offer (if any) this insight naturally connects to
- **Audience segment:** who this is aimed at

---

## Structure

### Subject line
Nurture subject lines work differently than promo subject lines. Prefer:
- Curiosity over benefit ("The $50K mistake I made at 28" beats "How to avoid costly mistakes")
- Specificity over generality ("What I learned from 1,000 budgets" beats "Lessons from my work")
- Conversational over polished ("I was wrong about this" beats "A surprising insight about X")

### Preview text
Extends the subject with a tease. Can be a question or a fragment of the story.

### Opening (1-2 paragraphs)
*Move:* drop the reader into the middle of the story, observation, or tension. Skip warm-up.

Options:
- Start mid-scene ("Last Tuesday, a student asked me...")
- Start with a concrete detail ("Her first salary offer was $85K. She negotiated to $140K.")
- Start with a question you're about to answer
- Start with a contrarian claim you'll defend

Avoid:
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I wanted to share..."
- "Today I want to talk about..."
- Any opener that announces what the email is about instead of doing it

### The body (the meat)

The structure depends on the content type. Choose one:

**Option A: Story-first**
1. The scene / what happened
2. The turn (the unexpected thing, the learning, the pivot)
3. The generalizable insight
4. What the reader can do with it

**Option B: Idea-first**
1. The claim (one sentence)
2. Why this matters
3. The evidence (story, data, example)
4. How to apply it

**Option C: Teardown**
1. What most people do
2. Why it doesn't work
3. What to do instead
4. Specific next step

Let the content pick the structure. Don't force Option A on a pure-idea email.

### The close (1-2 short paragraphs)
*Move:* land the insight. Options:
- One-line restatement of the core idea
- A question that makes the reader apply it to their own life
- A direct challenge or permission-giving statement

### Offer tie-in (optional, 1 line)
If — and only if — the offer connects naturally to the insight, include a single line. Not a pitch. Examples of what this looks like:

- "This is one of the things coaches work on in `{{OFFER}}`."
- "If this resonates, `{{OFFER}}` is where we go deep on it."
- "More on this inside `{{OFFER}}`: [link]"

If the insight doesn't connect naturally, skip this. Nurture emails without any offer mention are fine.

### Signature
Standard sign-off.

### PS (optional)
Good uses for a nurture PS:
- A second related thought
- A reader question you want to flag for next week
- A recommendation (book, podcast, tool) that fits the insight

---

## Length

- **Short nurture:** 150-300 words
- **Standard nurture:** 300-700 words
- **Long-form teardown:** 700-1,200 words (reserve for meaty teaching)

Nurture emails can run longer than promos because the reader is there to learn, not to decide. But shorter is still usually better.

---

## Rhythm and voice check

- Read out loud — nurture emails live or die on conversational flow
- Confirm every claim is sourceable (even in nurture emails, invented stats break trust)
- Run against `context/anti-ai-phrases.md`
- Em-dash count ≤ 1
- No rhetorical-question endings (see anti-ai-phrases.md)
- If the email could have been written by any brand in the category, it's too generic — add specificity
